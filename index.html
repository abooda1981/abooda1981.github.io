Introduction

FPV installations account for only a limited (~1%) of all solar PV globally, but at present they are receiving increased attention due to the possibility of making use of (expected) lower temperatures at or near water bodies as well as a favorable lack of shading regimens(Bank 2019a). In addition, a subset of authors are also paying attention to the possibilities afforded by deploying FPV offshore. Offshore FPV are particularly attractive for small, island nations

Objectives

Our work uses a simple, easy to reproduce model aimed at responding to three questions:

  1. Does the use of offshore FPV always lead to an improvement of performance of PV yield compared to land-based systems?
  2. For a given site on earth, can such an improvement be quantified?
  3. Does the quantification/definition of an “offshore advantage” correlate with a set of reasonable climate-related factors?

General Approach and Methods

A photovoltaic cell of a given type will have an efficiency \(\eta\) which is a direct function of the operating cell temperature and its own efficiency at Standard Testing Conditions, \(\eta_{STC}\) . For a monocrystalline Silicon cell, this can be modelled by: \[ \eta = \eta_{STC} *(1 - \beta * (T_{cell} - 25)) + \eta_{STC}* \gamma *Ilog_{10}(I) \] This cell temperature is in turn a function of both the ambient conditions surrounding the cell as well as the exact type of cell. Notably, the sensitivity of a PV cell to the ambient conditions, and therefore, the cell temperature, can change significantly for varying PV cell technologies. In our work, we simulate panels/modules which are composed entirely of mono-Si PV cells. Using long-term weather data, we model the monthly performance of an offshore FPV panel and compare it to a land-based PV panel which lies at a pre-determined distance of ~ 112 km. By defining the yield as $Y = G $ where \(G\) is the insolation incident on the panel, we can also define an “offshore advantage” which is the difference between the yield at the offshore site and at an associated inland site: \[ \delta = Y_{offshore} - Y_{inland} \]

Temperature advantages

One unexpected and early result was that there was no general rule which dictated that the offshore sites experienced temperatures lower than the inland sites. Indeed, in only 34.6% of the total number of months (12 months of average for each of 20 triplets) were the temperatures of the offshore sites colder than the temperatures of the associated inland site. This highlights the highly localized nature of how temperatures are distributed across the earth’s surface (including both land and open seas).

KG Classification and (F)PV Yield

Contrary to expectations, our results show that the Koeppen-Geiger (KG) climate zone classification system was not a meaningful predictor of PV yield. Specifically, when the monthly yields of PV panels were arranged in monthly values, a K-means clustering algorithm was unable to accurately classify the sites in accordance with the KG classification of the coastal site. Note that KG classifications for offshore sites were not forthcoming. In preparing this dataset, we also ensured that the monthly values for all given sites were arranged in the proper order for whether they were north or south of the equator; this meant that the sites followed a summer-winter pattern always, and prevented the confusion which would otherwise ensue if PV yields from June in Argentina, for example, were compared to PV yields for June in, say, Canada. Regression and offshore advantage

Our original results provided the PV yields in units of \(kWh/m^2\); making a number of simplifications, we then scaled up to the yield of a 1 \(MW_{p}\) of a solar PV plant, and defined a yield in MWh per year for each site. Finally, we calculated an “offshore advantage” in yields for each triplet as described above. The map below shows how this offshore advantage differs from one location to the next.

The amount by which an offshore FPV outperformed an associated inland site varied according to an intricate geographical pattern.

Next, we looked at ways to correlate the offshore advantage with a number of different climatological indicators. Unsurprisingly, there are a number of different regression models which can explain the data. Some of these are overly obvious and/or examples of overfitting, such as tying the differences in yield to differences in insolation. Instead, the following correlation gives the difference in (expected) daily yield, measured in kWh, as a function of the latitude of the coastal site (\(L\)), the difference in average daily temperature between the offshore and inland site, and the normalised difference in the clearness index between the sites, for a weighted annual average. The below expression

\[ Y_{day} = 0.02 - 0.03L - 0.02L^2 -0.1 \nabla T_{day, year} + \nabla K_{annual}^T\]

In general, the offshore advantage for PV yields followed a specific pattern and could be modelled with a high degree of certainty ( $ R^2 > 90% $). There were a number of models of varying levels of success, but our main result attempts to give an explanation which has a lot of explanatory power but is also not a foregone conclusion.

Economic impacts

Define $ LCOE $ as the difference in the LCOE between an offshore and an inland site. Our modelling indicates that this value will vary greatly by location. Specifically, within our dataset, we calculate that $ LCOE $ in a country like Vietnam may be as little as 30% of that for Malaysia. This is despite a much higher interest rate in the former (7.5% vs. 4.5%). This demonstrates how the same geographical constraints which define offshore FPV gains have economic impacts. Additional data for this economic modelling came from (Bank 2019b) and (Olivera-Pinto and Stokkermans 2020).
